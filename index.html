Definition & Scope of Epidemiology

  • Epidemiology – study of distribution and determinants of health‑related states in populations, and application to control health problems.
  • Focus: distribution (who, where, when) and determinants (causes, risk factors).
  • Investigates frequency (rates) and patterns (person, place, time).
  • Includes both disease and health events (e.g., injury, wellness).

Key components:

  • Descriptive epidemiology (person, place, time).
  • Analytic epidemiology (testing hypotheses).
  • Applied epidemiology (surveillance, outbreak investigation, evaluation).

Epidemiologic Triad & Chain of Infection

Epidemiologic Triad:

  • Agent – infectious agent, chemical, physical (e.g., bacteria, virus, toxin).
  • Host – human with susceptibility, immunity, behavior.
  • Environment – physical, biological, social conditions that allow transmission.

Chain of Infection:

  • Infectious agent → Reservoir → Portal of exit → Mode of transmission → Portal of entry → Susceptible host.

Modes of transmission:

  • Direct contact, droplet, airborne, vehicle‑borne (water, food), vector‑borne.
  • Breaking any link prevents disease.

Measures of Disease Frequency (Rates, Ratios)

MeasureFormulaInterpretation
Incidence rate(new cases / population at risk) × multiplierNew events over time (risk)
Prevalence rate(total existing cases / total population) × 100All cases at a point in time (burden)
Attack rate(ill / exposed) × 100Used in outbreaks
Crude death rate(deaths / total population) × 100,000General mortality
Infant mortality rate (IMR)(infant deaths / live births) × 1,000Indicator of population health
Maternal mortality ratio (MMR)(maternal deaths / live births) × 100,000Quality of obstetric care

Key distinction: Incidence = new cases (risk); Prevalence = old + new (burden).

Study Designs in Epidemiology

Descriptive studies:

  • Case reports, case series, cross‑sectional surveys.
  • Generate hypotheses.

Analytical studies:

  • Case‑control – retrospective, odds ratio.
  • Cohort – prospective/retrospective, relative risk.

Experimental studies:

  • Randomized controlled trials (RCTs), community trials.
  • Test interventions (e.g., vaccine efficacy).

Mnemonic: D‑A‑E = Descriptive → Analytical → Experimental (pyramid of evidence).

Disease Surveillance & Outbreak Investigation

  • Surveillance – ongoing systematic collection, analysis, interpretation, dissemination of health data.
  • NESSS – National Epidemic Sentinel Surveillance System (hospital‑based).
  • Notifiable diseases (e.g., AFP, measles, neonatal tetanus, rabies).
  • Outbreak investigation steps: prepare, verify diagnosis, define cases, descriptive epidemiology, develop hypotheses, evaluate, implement control, communicate findings.
  • Case definition: clinical, time, place, person.

Outbreak types: Sporadic, endemic, epidemic, pandemic, cluster.

PHN Role in Epidemiology

  • Surveillance: Report notifiable diseases (AFP, measles, etc.) using FHSIS and NESSS.
  • Data collection: Collect accurate morbidity/mortality data from home visits and clinics.
  • Outbreak response: Participate in case finding, contact tracing, specimen collection, health education.
  • Calculate rates: Compute incidence, prevalence, attack rates for local planning.
  • Interpret data: Use vital statistics to identify high‑risk groups.
  • Advocacy: Present epidemiological evidence to LHB for resource allocation.
  • Research: Conduct community surveys and assist in epidemiological studies.

Key role: The PHN is the frontline epidemiologist in the community – detecting signals, responding early.
